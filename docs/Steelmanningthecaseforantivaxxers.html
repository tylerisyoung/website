It is clear that covid is causing brain fog, and fatigue, which would seem clear from any illness, but it gets more interesting when considering the signs of aging, premature aging, one measurement of aging includes that of the fallability of the Blood-Brain barrier (BBB). Senatorov et al. explain the significance of this barrier in a study on mice; “Albumin was effectively absent from the hippocampus of young mice but was first detectable in the aging hippocampus starting as early as 12 months (“middle age”) and consistently elevated in aging up to 2 years, near the end of the life span. These results indicate that BBB dysfunction appears earlier than has been appreciated, placing it among the earliest known indicators of aging in the rodent brain." The study goes on to state that one of the major challenges in treating or delaying the onset of neurological disorders is that by the time they are recognized, the dysfunctions and degeneration are often no longer reversible (with treatments like fasting) and therefore treatment and prevention of further damage is also limited.

As early as April 30th, 2021 it was discovered that the spike protein causes damage to the lungs and other organs, regardless of origin, being natural infection, or the synthesis of the spike protein from vaccine in order to prime the immune system. Either method has the same resulting spike protein in our bloodstream and causes some level of immune response. Lei et al. find; “Although the use of a noninfectious pseudovirus is a limitation to this study, our data reveals that S protein alone can damage endothelium, manifested by impaired mitochondrial function and eNOS activity but increased glycolysis. It appears that S protein in ECs increases redox stress which may lead to AMPK deactivation, MDM2 upregulation, and ultimately ACE2 destabilization.” Endothelium are cells which line organs including the heart and all of our blood vessels, it was previously unknown, or unproven that covid spike protein impairs the functionality of the mitochondria of these cells. They conclude by saying; “By uncovering a foundational mechanism linking BBB decline to neural dysfunction, our study raises several critical questions: What causes BBB decline itself? Is BBB decline causal to, concurrent with, or an outcome of (or independent from) other well-known mecha- nisms of aging such as inflammation, reactive oxidation stress and metabolic failures, proteasome senescence, DNA damage, etc. … we show that BBB dysfunction may be an early step causing or contributing to activation of astrocytes and the ensuing inflammatory response. Similarly …We show that extravasation of albumin through the leaky BBB and astrocytic uptake may be one of the earliest steps that induce this age-related TGF cascade.” This conclusive connection between the loss in strength of the BBB and aging would naturally lead to our age related afflictions like that of alsheimers or stroke which are interrelated with the ACE2 receptor of cells.

Pena-Silva and Heistad suggest that the ACE2 receptor plays a protective role in the pathopsychology of cerebrovascular disease and stroke, their model suggests that shortly after a stroke, ACE2 activity in the brain increases, and for the days following, ACE2 continues to increase and remain elevated for three days, and they suggest that the presence of ACE2 is acting as a protective function of the brain. The opposite seems to be occuring in relation to alsheimers, in which ACE2 is not present, and therefore not providing any protective function for the brain, Kehoe et al. find, “ACE-2 enzyme activity is reduced in Alzheimer’s disease in association with increasing Aβ load and tau pathology ACE-2 activity was significantly reduced by approximately 50% in the mid-frontal cortex in AD compared with age-matched controls (P < 0.0001). ACE-2 varied according to disease severity” We therefore now can presume that ACE2 plays some still relatively unknown roll in protecting the brain itself.

An experiment on covid patients by Buzhdygan et al. cfound, “when compared to the control, the dementia cases had an average of 3.5 ± 0.29 fold increase in ACE2 expression and the hypertension cases indicated a 5.1 ± 0.32 fold in ACE2 expression. The results demonstrate that the main target of the SARS-CoV-2 spike protein is expressed in the brain vasculature and it also appears to be upregulated in underlying health conditions known to worsen COVID- 19 outcomes.” This tells us a number of things, first, that the the spike protein is crossing the blood brain barrier, and additionally that ACE2 is being upregulated in ways that are consistent with the time period following a storke.

Additionally, a paper published by Byram and Bridle on June 15, 2021 finds four major concerns with the spike protein, and allude to a fifth complication of the crossing of the blood brain barrier, "In lay language, proteins like the spike protein, not the intact virus, appear to mediate

much of the damage in the body in people who suffer from severe COVID-19. When the spike protein binds to these receptors, there are several events that can take place:

  1. Proteins (called ‘complement proteins’) that are part of our innate immune system can get activated, causing inflammation that can damage or destroy the cells lining blood vessels and/or platelets34. Platelets that are required for clotting of blood also express ACE2 receptors that can bind with spike protein with dire consequences. Damage and destruction of platelets can cause their numbers to go down (a condition known as “thrombocytopenia”), and if platelet counts get too low and blood vessels are damaged, bleeding cannot be stopped. Therefore, the spike protein can potentiate bleeding.

  2. Binding of the spike protein to platelets can also cause the platelets to become activated35. Activated platelets tend to clump, which can lead to the formation of clots. There is evidence that the spike protein can interact with other proteins in the blood to promote clotting36. As such, the spike protein can promote blood clotting.

  3. Spike proteins binding to the cells that line our blood vessels can cause these cells to express proteins (known as ‘caspases’) that can cause the cells to die33. This is similar to findings from the 2002-2004 SARS outbreak where the spike protein from the original SARS-CoV could cause cells to die when it was being manufactured inside of them37. Dying cells that have been manufacturing the vaccine-encoded spike protein would release free spike protein or portions thereof.

  4. Spike proteins binding to the cells that line our blood vessels can cause these cells to over- produce cell-signalling cytokines that can potentially contribute to dangerous ‘cytokine storms’ (overly robust and severe inflammation)33, 38.

Of additional concern is the knowledge that the spike protein is capable of dissociating into two parts and these smaller subunits (S1 and S2) can cross the blood-brain barrier where they can potentially cause damage in the brain39. Indeed, people who have died from severe COVID- 19 with neurological signs were found to have the spike proteins but not the intact virus in their brains40. These neurological signs could be seen in laboratory studies when spike proteins were injected into the blood of mice."
